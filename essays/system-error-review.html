Democracy in the Age of Technocrats

2023 · Book Review

How do we regulate big tech and keep our democracy safe? Three Stanford professors diagnose our societal ills and point to solutions in System Error.

The early tech industry, initially a wild enclave of free-floating ideas with aspirations to connect the world, has shifted to become an amalgamation of massive companies that appear hegemonic in the absence of opposition from formidable competitors or regulations. Ethicist Rob Reich, Computer Scientist Mehran Sahami and Political Scientist Jeremy Weinstein explain how “the utopian notions of technology as a great equalizer have given way to dystopian stories of data breaches, surveillance capitalism, biased algorithms, and rampant misinformation” (26). They explain the implications of rapid technological change for the upkeep of our democracy. 

The first section of the book covers the pitfalls of the optimization mindset adopted by many in the tech industry. The authors introduce the comical story of Soylent, a liquified, soy-based meal replacement developed by Silicon Valley Engineer Rob Rhinehart. The goal of the product was simple: to optimize the inefficiencies associated with meal prep and eating a nutritious meal quickly. The product surpassed its initial funding goals and took off. Soylent stands as a perfect example of the optimization mindset of a technologist. This mindset yields amazing results - much of the technology we use today would not be nearly as efficient without an eye for optimization, and that improves our lives - from the speed with which we launch apps, to the spot-on recommendations by streaming services. However, the authors caution that optimization should never be an end in itself, but in the technology industry, the pursuit of efficiency often eclipses all other considerations. 

Optimization is closely tied to other indicators of technological success - monetization, engagement, reach, and similar easy-to-measure metrics. The authors argue that in pursuing these measures, companies and platforms can lose sight of broader goals - whether the firm’s founding principles, or even the goals of a functioning democracy (if they even value these in the first place). The authors raise concerns regarding privacy, algorithmic decision making, automatization, free speech, and democratic discourse. They resurrect thinkers of the past to speak in conversation with technologists of the present. They employ famous thought experiments to illustrate their points - from Bentham’s panopticon to explain the psychological experience of mass surveillance to the trolley problem in describing the ethical implications of self-driving vehicles. By invoking these thought experiments, the authors lend a sense of timelessness to the technological ethical dilemmas we face today. 

They explain that Zuckerberg might be the new Rockefeller, but he can’t be one of Plato’s philosopher kings. In one example, the authors juxtapose John Maynard Keynes’ worry of technological unemployment, with Google’s chief economist’s assurance that AI will only replace jobs that are ‘dull, repetitive and unpleasant’ (174). As any college student as myself knows, the job market that I see now will be drastically different by the time I graduate, and even more so after my first job.

Although the chapter on AI and automation posits that these technologies may replace up to 40% of existing jobs, Reich, Sahami, and Weinstein remain hopeful. They believe in the power of government and new innovation to balance out major shifts in the balance of skilled and unskilled labor. They introduce Andrew Yang’s popularization of the idea of universal basic income, without prescribing this as the only way forward and leading us down a path towards the automated world of civilians and engineers envisioned by Kurt Vonnegut in his 1952 novel Player Piano.

The authors let actors like Zuckerberg speak for themselves. System Error is littered with quotes from professors to economists, to CEOs and businesspeople. This allows the authors to propose new ideas while avoiding the pitfalls of being overly prescriptive. However, while the construction of anecdote - quote - commentary - rhetorical question can begin to feel formulaic, each subject is thoroughly intriguing that this structure proves useful and not rote.

With interjections of personal experience (Rob was invited to a hush-hush dinner party with tech founders, Sahami was approached by a tech investor after overheard talking about search engines in a restaurant), System Error provides perspective from academics who have lived and breathed the tech industry for the majority of their careers. They provide insight into the ills of the industry, and present hope that future regulation will reaffirm democratic values.
