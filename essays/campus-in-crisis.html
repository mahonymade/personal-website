Campus in Crisis

On May 2nd, the Dartmouth College Green was flooded with campus security, local police, and state troopers. Co-opted by the authorities, college-owned vans typically employed to transport students to the nearby White Mountains instead filled with students headed to the local Lebanon police station. At Dartmouth, there were 92 arrests. Out of an undergraduate population of under 5,000, this means that nearly 2% of my peers were arrested during a protest that spanned one evening.

New president Sian Leah Beilock’s actions to stymie activists were brutally swift. Parts of campus were shocked as they coordinated cars to pick up their classmates before lectures the next morning.

As the death toll of the conflict inflates to over 40,000, the majority Gazans, erstwhile Israeli hostages continue to fall victim to Hamas brutality, the fall term will dictate whether this conflict remains top of mind or recedes to a low hum of discomfiting tragedy, eclipsed by blame games as Harris and Trump vie over control of the White House.

My prediction? Reactions to the war in Gaza will remain in stasis, slowly simmering unless a precipitating event turns up the heat. Like many social issues, a small but vocal minority attracts the majority of attention. When I called friends to hear about the political temperature, the most common report was one of apathetic calm. The majority of students are not out on the green or holding signs. They are holding court over library desks, confined to their own campus sized kingdoms of academic scholarship (or survival), unawares (unconcerned) of the brutality half a world away.
