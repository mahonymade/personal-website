Review: The Long Fix

2023 · Book Review

Radiologist and healthcare executive Vivian Lee diagnoses the ills of the U.S. healthcare system, and proposes solutions that might just be the (long) fix.

Our healthcare system is crippled with inefficiencies - from surgeons who are stuck searching through patient records on hospital time to thousands of excess expensive MRI orders, it is unsurprising that the U.S. spends more than any other nation on healthcare - nearly $4 trillion every year. Lee laments: “this ailing, failing system is making our nation sick - financially, emotionally, and physically.” (7) 

Yet with her impressive credentials - Lee was educated at Harvard, Oxford, Duke, and NYU, has headed the University of Utah health system, and is now a clinical executive at Google, Lee attempts to boil down the mire that is our overcomplicated healthcare system into a mere 200 pages that is accessible to someone without any of her advanced degrees. Lee chooses to focus on the financials of U.S. healthcare, but explains how they in turn drive the whole system. Though discussing cutting costs in healthcare can feel uncomfortable, Lee assures the reader that restructuring our healthcare spending will cut costs while improving patient outcomes.

Early in The Long Fix, Lee recounts a story from former US Secretary of Health and Human Services Alex Azar, who describes his experience with a high deductible plan. His physician recommends that he undergo a routine echocardiogram stress test, Azar agrees, thinking he would receive the test in his physician's office. Unexpectedly, he is admitted to the hospital, where the list price of the procedure is $5,500. His health insurance negotiated price is $3500. In the end, he discovers that the test would have cost only $550 if it was performed in his physician's office. 

This anecdote demonstrates business as usual in the ‘pay-for-action’ model, and the lack of transparency in medical pricing. While Azar’s care could have been much less expensive, his physician and hospital benefit from driving up costs instead of trying to cut them down. The hospital makes more money the more surgeries it hosts, the more tests its physicians order, and the more procedures its staff perform. In this model, the patient’s health is not a priority. In fact, the hospital stands to make more money the sicker the patient is, and the sooner they return. While no hospital under this system wishes harm to its patients, there is little financial incentive for hospitals to improve care or innovate at the rate of other private sector companies. 

Lee champions a ‘pay-for-results’ model, in which hospitals are paid a fixed fee by insurers for certain common procedures, and are rewarded when their patients meet benchmarks such as being complication-free 90 days after a procedure. Lee points to her own success in heading the University of Utah health system to support this model. Between 2013 and 2015, and driven by the Value Driven Outcome initiative, “the University of Utah health system reduced expenses and doubled its operating margin to 10%, considerably higher than the national average of 2-3%” (99). By cutting costs, hospitals that adopt this system are able to detach themselves from the “zero gravity economics” (119) that dominates the U.S. healthcare system. The new model incentivizes hospitals to inspect the quality and efficiency of their care, and gives doctors the incentive to explore non-medical interventions to improve patient health (such as diet, exercise, community participation) that may be more effective than any course of treatment.

From my own encounters with the U.S. medical system, I know that Lee does not exaggerate in describing the magnitude of overtreatment. I’ve been prescribed antibiotics for a sore throat, an MRI for a small bout of knee pain, and when I tore my ACL, the possibility of foregoing surgery to pursue physical therapy instead was treated as an afterthought and not a real option.

The ambition of The Long Fix matches that of its well-credentialed author. However, I found myself still puzzled after certain chapters, as the sheer volume of information Lee has to share is enormous. Nevertheless, Lee distills down the sprawling ills of American healthcare spending and inefficiency into twelve distinct chapters, which each finish with a list of recommendations for patients, medical workers, insurers, and policymakers. The Long Fix empowers its reader to question the status quo of our medical system, and demand better.
