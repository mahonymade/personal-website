Iran and the Brink of Conflict

Position Memo · 2022

What is pushing Iran and its adversaries to the brink of conflict? And what would bring them back and create a minimum of security in the Arab/Persian Gulf?

Iran seems to be hurtling towards a future rife with conflict, both externally and internally. Iran’s internal politics drive the state to be aggressive and hostile towards groups that do not hold the same beliefs as its current Islamist authoritarian regime. Externally, this hostility is reflected most strongly towards Israel, Iraq, and Syria, and internally the regime seeks to extinguish any protest or reformist activity that challenges its status quo. I will explore Iran’s relationship with Israel and the U.S., as well as attitudes towards the Green Movement.

Currently, Iran is beset with protests demanding justice for the death of an Iranian woman, Mahsa Amini, who died while in police custody (Guardian). She was beaten to death after being arrested by the morality police for failing to wear a head covering. Many Iranians have taken to the streets to protest this tragedy and the regime. The UN office of the high commissioner for human rights reports that more than 300 people have been killed so far in Iran’s crackdown (Guardian). While this recent tragedy has set off a new wave of discontent with the regime, the regime’s brutal response to the protests is not novel.

Of particular interest are the protests of 2009, following the disputed election of Mahmoud Ahmadinejad in the place of the reformist candidate Hossein Mousavi. Nicknamed the “Green Movement,” these protestors clamored for a more moderate, liberal approach to Iranian politics. The protestors were met with gunfire and heavy repression by the Iranian government. The IRGC (Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps), a militia group who reports directly to the Supreme Leader, figured prominently in containing protests both in 2009 and now (AP). In addition to physical force, the Iranian government also works in the digital realm to constrain the free speech of its citizens. The Iranian government keeps a robust censorship network, which further limits the exchange of ideas within their tightly controlled populace (AP).

The IRGC has increasingly entered the political sphere, following its origins as a protective force of the ideas of the 1979 Iranian revolution. In 2005, four of the seven presidential candidates had served in the IRGC, and currently 24 members of the Iranian parliament have served in the force (MEI). The IRGC is a formidable military force, with over 100,000 ground troops, 20,000 navy forces, and control of strategic air defense missiles (CFR). In 2019, the U.S. declared the IRGC a foreign terrorist organization (CFR), a declaration that has thrown a wrench in U.S.-Iran talks to reinstate the JCPOA (Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action).

The JCPOA has become an increasingly hot issue in the region. After the Trump administration suspended the deal in 2018, the Biden administration has been working to reimplement it. The JCPOA gave Iran hundreds of billions of dollars of relief from tariffs and sanctions imposed by the U.S. in response to Iran’s nuclear program. The reimplementation of U.S. tariffs magnified the failings of Iran’s already struggling economy. Iran’s economy shrunk by almost 7% in 2019, and its unemployment rate stood at 11% (MEI). In addition, while sanctions hurt the Iranian economy generally, they may serve as a boon to the IRGC, who thrives off of the black market and smuggling trade for goods such as oil, cash, narcotics, and consumer goods. This black market only expands in response to U.S. sanctions (FP).

Externally, Iran is interested in promoting Islam abroad as well as protecting the people of Palestine, which it sees as oppressed by Israel. In an analysis of speeches by former Iranian president Hassan Rouhani by the Tony Blair Institute for Global Change, Rouhani referenced pan-Islamist ideas with support for Islamist militias abroad in 60% of his speeches from 2014 to 2018 (Tony Blair Institute). In addition, the Institute analyzed speeches by Khamenei, Khomeini, and other political figures in regards to their rhetoric on Israel. They found that anti-Israel rhetoric featured in 66% of the sample, and “present in speeches of all leaders analyzed.” The institute noted Supreme Leader Khamenei’s particular vitriol towards Israel, where he calls Israel “a cancerous tumor that must be eradicated,” and anti-Israel rhetoric populates 90% of his speeches (Tony Blair Institute). On the other side of the conflict, the Israel Democracy Institute fielded a survey that asked to what extent Israelis saw Iran as an existential threat. 54% of respondents responded to a “great or very great extent,” while 32% responded to a “medium or small extent” (Israel Democracy Institute). These figures demonstrate the depth of fear and distrust between the two nations. Without the U.S. involvement in the JCPOA, it would not be surprising for Iran to continue to pursue nuclear weapons to match Israel’s well-endowed and well-advertised nuclear weapons capabilities.

Another step forward for Iran would be to find a way to reopen online communication in the country, and decrease or eliminate the power of censorship of the Iranian government. By stifling free speech online, Iran is also stifling the organization of reformist movements.

The path forward for a peaceful Iran is twofold. For the time being, to assuage security concerns in the region and deescalate the threat of a nuclear Iran, the U.S. should continue talks to reenter the JCPOA Iran nuclear deal. This would be more effective than the imposition of further sanctions, which do not appear to disincentivize nuclearization.

Iran’s salvation may lie in finding a way for reform movements like the Green Party to generate their own political standing. However, within the oppressive Iranian system, it is unlikely that a Green Party member could be elected, even if they had the popular support of the people. A candidate from any reform movement is unlikely to receive the stamp of approval from the Guardian Council.
