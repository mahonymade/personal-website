State Collapse in Libya

Position Memo · 2022

How do you explain state collapse in Libya after the 2011 uprising? And why has it been difficult to put it back together?

In the vacuum created by the fall of the Qaddafi regime, the Libyan state failed to have a clean-cut transition of power to a new regime. The transition of power was complicated by the brutality (and strength) of the rebel forces as well as the lack of consensus by interested foreign powers. While internal factors such as tribalism and national identity are also important, I will focus on outside powers’ influence on Libya, and how they affect the progression of its interior politics.

Up through the end of fighting, there were still brutal clashes for the city of Tripoli between Qaddafi loyalists and rebel forces. Both groups were investigated and found to have committed war crimes against the other side, although a UN commission deemed the crimes committed by Qaddafi supporters to be much more severe (Britannica). The short timeline of the uprising as well as the UN-mediated outcome left the state in disarray. On September 15, only a few short months after the initial outbreak of revolution, and after significant international intervention, the U.N. General Council voted to recognize Libya’s rebel-led Transitional National Council (TNC) as the representative governing body of Libya (Britannica). However, despite this declaration, loyalists to the Qaddafi regime remained.

A weighty obstacle to the reunification of Libya’s government exists also in disparate international interests in the region. With its significant oil reserves, Libya represents an important strategic battleground in the search for non-Russian hydrocarbons. Following the crisis in Ukraine, many states are seeking to purchase oil from sources other than Russia, and Libya is of particular interest as an alternate distributor. As a result, it is rumored that countries like the U.S., France, and Egypt tacitly support the Bashagha regime, even as they officially recognize Dabaiba’s government in Tripoli (Crisis Group). Bashagha’s supporters have greater control of the oil fields that finance the country than the Tripoli government.

Already, Libya is struggling to pay for social programs and other infrastructure programs, and relies on foreign aid to function. To address this issue, international actors have proposed creating a third party bank system that would administer Libyan oil rent funds until the state is able to coalesce to create a unity government, or one of the two executives triumphs over the other. While foreign powers are interested in finding stability for Libya to ensure that they can purchase oil from the state, no overarching consensus exists on the best mechanism to achieve this.

In addition to concerns about the ability to purchase oil from Libya, many foreign states have a stake in the emergence of Islamist groups in the state. Without centralized control of the country, smaller groups and militias have been holding power in the region. As a result, jihadist groups like “Islamic State” (IS) have proliferated, adding to increasing instability. Geographically, Libya is a convenient place to allow for Islamist movement to neighbors Algeria and Egypt (JSTOR 24). Additionally, with the weakening of Islamist power in Iraq and Syria, Libya has become a safe harbor for an estimated 500 to several thousand jihadist Salafists (INSS 48).

The influx of Islamist extremism into Libya has been cause for concern for states like Egypt, but presented itself as an opportunity to spread Islamist ideology for others. Since 2011, Qatar and Turkey have channeled weapons to their Islamist allies within Libya, who have operated under the moniker “Libyan Dawn” (INSS 47). Due to the collapse of the Libyan state, some Libyan army weapons depots were looted, generating a weapons smuggling network reaching to the Sinai peninsula, the Gaza strip, and into Egypt (INSS 48). To combat this weapons flow, the UAE has delivered weapons to Field Marshal Khalifa Haftar and his allies who oppose the Muslim Brotherhood. Egypt has supported these activities in an effort to stem political Islam in the region.

From these concerns, multiple possible courses of action have emerged. The internationally recognized interim government of Abdelhamid Dabaiba based out of Tripoli is committed to staying in power until parliamentary elections are held, despite the lack of an approved constitution. Former interior minister Fathi Bashagha is in power due to a contested vote. Based out of the city of Sirt, Bashagha demands that Dabaiba step aside, and hopes to draft an amended constitution before holding simultaneous legislative and presidential elections in 2023 (Crisis Group). Increasingly, Bashagha has threatened and attempted (twice) to storm Tripoli in hopes of capturing the capital. His failure to do so (at the cost of at least 32 civilian lives) (Carnegie) has cost him some support in the Libyan parliament. Some European states have also cooled in their view of Bashagha’s government due to involvement of the Russian Wagner Group with Field Marshal Khalifa Haftar. However, at the same time, Turkey has lost some confidence in its support for Dabaiba due to Dabaiba’s relatively small sphere of influence, mainly centered in Tripoli (Carnegie).

Thus, due to Libya’s economic importance in the international oil market, as well as its strategic importance as a possible harbor for radical Islamists, the question of Libya’s reunification has swiftly become an international security issue.
